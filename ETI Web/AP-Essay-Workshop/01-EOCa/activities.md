# Rung 1: Guided Source Analysis (EOCa)

## Workshop Overview

**Skills in focus:** Identifying an author's argument, tracing their line of reasoning, and evaluating evidence effectiveness.

**What you'll need:** A pen or pencil, highlighters (3 colors if possible), and this packet.

---

## Bridge-In

Welcome to the first rung of the ladder.

Everything you do this year in essay writing starts here, with three deceptively simple questions about any text you read:

1. **What** is the author arguing?
2. **How** do they build that argument — what is their reasoning?
3. **Does their evidence actually work?**

These three questions are the backbone of the AP Seminar End-of-Course Exam (Part A). But more than that, they are the foundation for every analytical essay you will ever write. You cannot respond to an argument you haven't understood. You cannot critique reasoning you haven't mapped. You cannot build your own case if you can't tell strong evidence from weak evidence.

Today is hands-on. You will practice each skill separately, then put them together on a new passage under timed conditions. Let's get started.

---

## Activity 1 — Argument Hunting

### The Skill

An author's argument (or thesis) is the central claim the entire text is built to support. It is not the topic. It is not a fact. It is the author's *position* — the thing they want you to believe or consider by the end of the piece.

**Quick test:** If someone asked "So what's the point?" after reading the text, the answer is the argument.

### Practice Round 1 — Warm-Up (Easy)

Read the following short passage, then write the author's argument in one sentence.

> Travel is often sold as a luxury, but it may be closer to a necessity. Studies in psychology consistently show that exposure to unfamiliar environments increases cognitive flexibility, reduces implicit bias, and improves problem-solving skills. A two-week trip abroad does more for a student's intellectual development than a month of textbook study on the same region. Schools that cut travel programs in the name of budget savings may be cutting one of the most effective educational tools they have.

**Your answer:** What is the author's argument?

> **Guidance:** The author argues that travel is a powerful educational tool and that schools should not cut travel programs. Notice how the passage is not just *about* travel — it takes a *position*: travel is educationally valuable and cutting it is a mistake. If you wrote something like "the passage is about travel," push yourself further. What does the author *want you to believe* about travel?

---

### Practice Round 2 — Moderate Difficulty

> Most people assume that memory works like a video camera — recording events faithfully and playing them back on demand. Decades of research in cognitive science tell a different story. Every time we recall an event, we reconstruct it, filling in gaps with expectations, emotions, and information acquired after the fact. Eyewitness testimony, long treated as gold-standard evidence in courtrooms, is particularly vulnerable to this reconstructive process. The question is not whether our memories are unreliable. They are. The question is what we do with legal and social systems built on the assumption that they are not.

**Your answer:** What is the author's argument?

> **Guidance:** This one is trickier because the author builds to the argument gradually. The thesis is not "memory is unreliable" — that is presented as established fact. The argument is in the final sentence: our legal and social systems are built on a false assumption about memory's reliability, and we need to reckon with that. A strong answer captures both the *claim* (memory is reconstructive and unreliable) and the *so what* (systems that depend on memory need to be re-examined).

### Practice Round 3 — Harder (Implicit Thesis)

> In 1950, there were roughly 2.5 billion people on Earth. Today there are over 8 billion. Global grain production has more than tripled in that same period. The average person alive today has access to more calories than the average person in 1950. Yet approximately 735 million people are classified as chronically undernourished. Meanwhile, roughly one third of all food produced globally is lost or wasted before it is eaten. The technology to feed every person on the planet exists. The infrastructure to distribute food to every region has been built. The political will to do so has not.

**Your answer:** What is the author's argument?

> **Guidance:** The author never writes "I argue that..." This is an *implicit* thesis — the argument emerges from the arrangement of facts. The author is arguing that global hunger is not a problem of production or technology but of political will. Notice how the last three sentences are structured: "The technology exists. The infrastructure has been built. The political will has not." That final pivot is where the argument lives. When you encounter passages without an explicit thesis statement, look for the turn — the moment where facts stop being neutral and start pointing somewhere.

## Activity 2 — Reasoning Mapper

### The Skill

An author's **line of reasoning** is the sequence of claims they make to move you from where you are (before reading) to where they want you to be (accepting their argument). Think of it as a path: each claim is a stepping stone, and your job is to map the path.

### The Passage

Read the following passage from Julian D. Michels's *A Conscious History of Consciousness*:

> Modern humans tend to imagine reality as made of things: a chair, a cup, a tree. However, both wisdom teachings throughout time and cutting edge theories in physics have in common an insight that "things" are probably not what reality is really made of. What we call "things" are more like temporary shapes taken by an underlying field or ocean of possibility. The ancient Greek philosopher Heraclitus called this underlying reality "Fire" — not literal flame, but a metaphor for constant change and transformation. The Chinese philosopher Laozi used a different word — "Tao" — but pointed at the same idea: a formless, flowing source beneath the world of appearances. Modern physics arrives at a strikingly similar picture. At the subatomic level, what we think of as solid "stuff" looks more like temporary waves or fluctuations in an underlying probability field. The chair, the cup, the tree — these are not the fundamental story. They are ripples.

#### Reasoning Map — Completed Model

| Step | Claim | Evidence / Support | Connection to Next Step |
|------|-------|--------------------|------------------------|
| 1 | Humans naturally perceive reality as made of distinct "things." | Examples: a chair, a cup, a tree (common-sense experience). | Sets up the assumption the author will challenge. The word "however" signals the turn. |

| 3 | Ancient philosophy described reality as a flowing, formless source beneath appearances. | Heraclitus's "Fire" (constant change/transformation); Laozi's "Tao" (formless, flowing source). | Establishes the idea historically, then bridges to the modern era for parallel support. |
| 4 | Modern physics arrives at a similar picture. | At the subatomic level, "stuff" looks like waves or fluctuations in a probability field. | Shows convergence across time and disciplines, reinforcing the argument's credibility. |
| 5 | Everyday objects are not the fundamental reality — they are temporary patterns ("ripples"). | Returns to the opening examples (chair, cup, tree) and reframes them. | Closes the loop: the common-sense view from Step 1 is directly challenged and replaced. |

### Debrief Questions

- How does Michels use the opening examples (chair, cup, tree) structurally? *(They appear in Step 1 and return in Step 5, creating a frame.)*
- Why does Michels present ancient philosophy *before* modern physics? *(It establishes historical depth and makes the physics feel like confirmation rather than novelty.)*
- Where is the argument most dependent on a single move? *(Step 2 — the claim that ancient and modern views converge is the hinge. If that connection doesn't hold, the structure weakens.)*

## Activity 3 — Evidence Evaluator

### The Skill

Not all evidence is created equal. Your job as a reader is to ask four questions about any piece of evidence an author uses:

| Question | What You're Looking For |
|----------|------------------------|
| **What kind of evidence is it?** | Data/statistics? Expert testimony? Historical example? Analogy? Personal anecdote? |
| **How specific is it?** | Does the author name sources, give dates, cite studies — or stay vague? |
| **What's missing?** | What would make this evidence stronger? What has the author left out? |
| **Does it actually support the claim?** | Is the connection between evidence and claim tight, or is there a logical gap? |

| Chair, cup, tree examples | Illustrative analogy / common experience | Low — no source needed (appeals to shared intuition) | Nothing missing here — its purpose is to set up a contrast, not to prove anything. | Yes — effectively establishes the common-sense view the author wants to challenge. |
| Heraclitus's "Fire" | Historical / philosophical reference | Low — names the philosopher and the concept but provides no textual citation, date, or context. | A quote from Heraclitus, historical context (when/where), explanation of how scholars interpret "Fire." | Partially — it shows that *an* ancient thinker had this idea, but the brief treatment makes it hard to assess whether the comparison is accurate. |
| Laozi's "Tao" | Historical / philosophical reference | Low — same issue as Heraclitus. Names the concept but doesn't develop it. | Textual citation, cultural context, acknowledgment that "Tao" carries a wide range of meanings. | Partially — same limitation. The parallel is asserted more than demonstrated. |
| Subatomic physics description | Scientific reference (general) | Low — describes the idea ("waves in a probability field") but names no specific theory, experiment, or physicist. | Name of a theory (quantum field theory, wave-particle duality), a specific experiment, or a cited physicist. | Partially — the description is suggestive but unsubstantiated. A skeptical reader would want to see actual science, not a summary of it. |

### Key Takeaway

Michels's evidence has **impressive breadth** — it spans thousands of years and crosses from philosophy to physics. But it lacks **depth**. Each piece of evidence is referenced briefly without enough detail for a reader to verify or fully understand the claims. A stronger version of this passage would either go deeper into fewer examples or provide citations that let readers follow up.

### Strong vs. Weak Evidence — Comparison Exercise

Below are two versions of a claim with supporting evidence. Read both, then answer the questions that follow.

**Claim:** Sleep deprivation significantly impairs academic performance.

**Version A (Weak Evidence):**
> Everyone knows that not sleeping enough makes it harder to do well in school. Students who stay up late tend to get worse grades. Sleep is important for learning and memory.

**Version B (Strong Evidence):**
> A 2019 study published in *Science of Learning* tracked 600 university students over a semester and found that students who averaged fewer than six hours of sleep per night scored, on average, 1.5 grade points lower than peers who slept seven or more hours. The researchers controlled for variables including course difficulty, prior GPA, and caffeine consumption. Neuroscience research further explains the mechanism: during sleep, the hippocampus replays and consolidates newly encoded information, a process critical to long-term memory formation.

**Questions:**

1. Using the four-question framework, evaluate Version A. What kind of evidence does it use? How specific is it? What's missing?
2. Do the same for Version B.
3. Version A is not *wrong* — sleep deprivation does impair performance. So why is it weak evidence? What does this tell you about the difference between being *correct* and being *well-supported*?

> **Key insight for Question 3:** Being correct is not enough. An argument's job is to *convince a skeptical reader*. Version A asks you to take the author's word for it. Version B gives you something to check, challenge, and build on. In academic writing, evidence must be verifiable and specific — not just true.

## Practice Prompt — Timed Writing

You now have all three skills. Time to put them together.

**Directions:** Read the passage below. Then answer the three questions that follow. You have **25 minutes**.

### Passage

> The global decline in insect populations has received far less public attention than the decline of large mammals or coral reefs, but its consequences may be more severe. Insects pollinate roughly 75 percent of the world's flowering plants, including many crops that humans depend on for food. They decompose organic matter, recycling nutrients back into soil. They form the base of food webs that support birds, freshwater fish, and small mammals. A 2019 meta-analysis examining 73 studies across multiple continents found that insect biomass is declining at an average rate of 2.5 percent per year — a figure that, if sustained, would mean a halving of insect life within three decades. The primary drivers identified by researchers include habitat loss from agricultural expansion, widespread pesticide use (particularly neonicotinoids), light pollution that disrupts nocturnal insect behavior, and climate change altering seasonal timing. Some scientists have described the trend as an "insect apocalypse," though others caution that data gaps — especially in tropical regions where insect diversity is highest — make sweeping conclusions premature. What is not in dispute is that the systems humans depend on for food, clean water, and stable ecosystems are deeply entangled with the fate of creatures most people overlook entirely.

### Questions

1. **Identify the author's argument.** What is the central claim of this passage? State it in one clear sentence.

2. **Explain the author's line of reasoning.** How does the author build the argument? Trace the sequence of claims and explain how they connect to each other.

3. **Evaluate the effectiveness of the evidence.** What kinds of evidence does the author use? How specific and credible is it? What are its strengths? What, if anything, is missing or could be stronger?

---

### Response Guidance (Review After Writing)

**Question 1 — Argument:**
The author argues that the global decline in insect populations deserves far more attention because insects are essential to ecological and agricultural systems that humans depend on. A strong response captures both parts: the decline is serious *and* it is underappreciated.

**Question 2 — Line of Reasoning:**
The author follows a deliberate sequence: (1) frames insect decline as under-discussed relative to other environmental crises; (2) establishes why insects matter by listing their ecological roles (pollination, decomposition, food web support); (3) presents quantitative evidence for the decline (2.5% per year, potential halving in 30 years); (4) identifies causes (habitat loss, pesticides, light pollution, climate change); (5) acknowledges a counterpoint (data gaps, "premature" conclusions); (6) closes by reasserting the core claim — regardless of debate, human systems depend on insects. Notice the concession in step 5: it strengthens the argument by showing the author is aware of limitations.

**Question 3 — Evidence Evaluation:**
*Strengths:* The passage uses a published meta-analysis with specific numbers (73 studies, 2.5% decline, 30-year projection). It names a specific pesticide class (neonicotinoids). It acknowledges data limitations, which builds credibility. *Areas to strengthen:* The meta-analysis is described generally — the author could name the journal, lead researcher, or publication. The phrase "some scientists" is vague. The ecological roles (pollination percentage, decomposition, food webs) are presented as facts without citations — strong responses note that even well-known facts benefit from sourcing in academic writing.

## Self-Assessment

Before you turn in your practice response, evaluate it against these questions. Be honest — this is for your own growth.

| # | Reflection Question | Check |
|---|---------------------|-------|
| 1 | **Argument:** Did I state the author's argument as a specific *claim* (not just a topic)? Does my sentence capture the author's position, not just the subject matter? | |
| 2 | **Reasoning:** Did I trace the *sequence* of claims, or did I just list ideas from the passage without showing how they connect? A reasoning map should feel like a path, not a pile. | |
| 3 | **Evidence — Specificity:** When I evaluated the evidence, did I name *specific* pieces of evidence from the passage, or did I speak in generalities ("the author uses good evidence")? | |
| 4 | **Evidence — Balance:** Did I identify both strengths *and* limitations of the evidence? One-sided evaluation (all praise or all criticism) is almost always incomplete. | |
| 5 | **Evidence — "So What?":** Did I explain *why* the evidence strengths and weaknesses matter for the argument's overall persuasiveness, or did I just list them? | |
